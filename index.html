Business

Understand what DealerKey is, how the model works, how we make money, and what clients are agreeing to before we ever bid on their behalf.

What DealerKey Is

DealerKey is a licensed buyer's agent that gets everyday people access to dealer-only wholesale auctions — so they can buy the same cars dealers buy, at the same prices, without needing a dealer license.

How We Get Paid — The Total Purchase Obligation

When a client wins a bid, they owe the full Total Purchase Obligation. This is not just "the car price" — it's everything stacked together:

  • Hammer price — the winning auction bid
  • Auction buyer's fee — set by the auction, not us
  • DealerKey flat service fee — our cut, $1,500
  • Transport fee — if delivery is outside our local service area
  • Utah sales/use tax — at the rate when title transfers
  • DMV registration and title fees
  • Optional post-sale inspection — if the client requests one (and we strongly recommend they do)

Reps should always frame the $1,500 flat fee as our cost — the rest are pass-through costs the client would pay no matter who sourced the car.

The 25% Deposit

Before we bid on anything, the client pays a deposit equal to 25% of their max approved bid. This is the single most important policy point reps need to communicate clearly and early.

  • If we don't win a bid — fully refunded within 5 business days of the BAF (Bid Authorization Form) expiring. No fees, no deductions.
  • If we win a bid — the deposit becomes non-refundable and applies toward the Total Purchase Obligation. The client's commitment is locked in the moment the hammer falls.
  • If the client backs out before a bid is placed — non-refundable. Same if they gave us specs that made sourcing impossible.

This is why the BAM (Bid Authorization Meeting) matters so much — it's the moment a client moves from "interested" to "committed."

The Client Lifecycle

1

Every new lead's first goal is getting them on a brief Google Meet — the IAM. Their willingness to show up for this call is itself a signal of how serious and committed they are.

After the IAM, the client gets sorted:

  • Searching — ready to actively look now
  • Qualifying / Needs Time — waiting on financing or other life stuff before committing

For "Needs Time" clients: customize check-in cadence to their situation, stay on their radar without pressure, and periodically confirm where they're at.

For "Searching" clients, the rep sends example listings a minimum of 2-3 times per week — more for most clients. Every listing update includes the rep's opinion on the best option that week, while leaving the decision completely up to the client.

Non-listing contact includes answering questions, checking preferences, and following up. The goal: involved, communicative, consistent — never overbearing. Match cadence to the client's demeanor.

As-Is Purchase — What Reps Need to Know

Every vehicle is purchased as-is, where-is. Clients can't physically inspect or test-drive before the auction run. Condition reports come from third-party graders, not us. This is a real risk the client is accepting — reps shouldn't downplay it, but also shouldn't make it scary.

The best move: recommend the post-sale inspection on every transaction. It's a small pass-through cost that gives the client real peace of mind, and it's the honest answer when someone asks "what if something's wrong with the car?"

The Vehicle Purchase Agreement — Key Points

This is the document sent and reviewed during the BAM. Reps don't need to memorize every clause, but should be able to clearly explain:

  • We're the buyer's agent — we don't own the car, don't mark it up, don't take kickbacks
  • The BAF (Bid Authorization Form) — separate from the agreement, specifies the car, max bid, delivery ZIP, and expires in 30 days
  • 25% deposit — refundable if no bid is won, non-refundable once we win
  • Balance due — within 3 business days of a winning bid, by wire/ACH/cashier's check (no personal checks)
  • As-is purchase — no warranties from DealerKey; post-sale inspection strongly recommended
  • Financing is the client's responsibility — must be secured before the BAF is signed
  • Transport damage — claims go to the carrier, not DealerKey; client must note damage on the bill of lading at delivery

Vehicle Fundamentals

Reps need to speak fluently about drivetrain, powertrain type, and trim levels — these come up constantly in client conversations. The reference below has the full detail; the key logic is summarized here.

  • Drivetrain — how power gets from the engine to the wheels. The main types: FWD, RWD, AWD, 4WD.
  • AWD vs. 4WD — AWD is always-on, automatic, built for everyday traction in rain/snow (most crossovers and SUVs). 4WD is driver-engaged (or auto-engaging on newer trucks), built for low-traction off-road or towing situations — found on trucks and body-on-frame SUVs like the 4Runner, Tahoe, Wrangler.
  • Hybrid vs. Plug-in Hybrid (PHEV) — a regular hybrid never plugs in; the gas engine and battery work together automatically and you fuel it like normal. A PHEV has a larger battery that can be charged from a wall outlet, giving a short all-electric range (often 20-40 miles) before it behaves like a regular hybrid.
  • Trim levels — generally a ladder from base → mid → upper-mid → top, where each step typically adds: better interior materials, more tech/infotainment, more driver-assist safety features, upgraded wheels, and sometimes a different engine or drivetrain option. It's not exact across brands, but the logic holds.

Manheim & Auction Mechanics

Arbitration light system — every Manheim listing carries a colored light/label that tells you the arbitration status of the vehicle:

  • Green — full arbitration rights. This is our default. If something material is misrepresented (odometer, frame damage, undisclosed accidents, etc.), we can file for arbitration after the sale.
  • Yellow — limited arbitration, usually tied to a specific announced issue (e.g. a known mechanical problem disclosed at sale). Arbitration rights apply to everything except what was announced.
  • Red — sold "as-is," essentially zero arbitration rights. We only bid red for a client if they explicitly understand and accept that there is no recourse if something is wrong — no exceptions, no assumptions.
  • Blue — typically indicates a vehicle sold with a specific certification or program backing (varies by sale), often with its own separate guarantee terms outside the standard arbitration policy. Always check the specific terms before treating it like a standard green light.

Default rule for reps: bid green. If a client is dead-set on a red-light vehicle, the conversation about zero arbitration rights has to happen clearly, out loud, before the BAM — not buried in the paperwork.

Out-of-state vehicles and post-sale inspection — for any vehicle being shipped out of state, strongly encourage the post-sale inspection (~$150). The client can't see the car in person, transport adds its own risk, and $150 is cheap insurance against a much more expensive surprise.

AutoCheck vs. CarFax — Manheim listings may show either or both. Reps should be able to explain both simply to clients:

  • CarFax — the more widely recognized vehicle history report. Pulls from a broad network of DMVs, insurance companies, service shops, and auctions to show accident history, ownership changes, title issues, service records, and odometer readings over time.
  • AutoCheck — Experian's version of the same idea. Slightly different data sources and network (heavily used in the auction/dealer world), gives a "score" summarizing the vehicle's history, and covers similar categories — accidents, title issues, odometer, ownership history.

Bottom line for clients: these are two different companies pulling from overlapping but not identical data sources. Neither is "better" — having both gives a fuller picture, and a clean report from one doesn't guarantee the other is clean too.

Tire grades — auction condition reports usually list tire tread depth as a fraction or percentage (e.g. "7/32" or "70%"), sometimes per tire. New tires are roughly 10-11/32". Anything in the 2-3/32" range is near end-of-life and likely needs replacing soon — factor that into how a deal is presented (it's a real, near-term cost the client should know about).

Transmission types — reps should recognize the basics when they show up in listings:

  • Automatic (AT) — standard torque-converter automatic, the most common.
  • CVT (Continuously Variable Transmission) — common on Honda/Toyota compact and midsize models for fuel efficiency; no fixed "gears," smooth and gradual power delivery.
  • Manual (MT) — driver-operated clutch and gear selection; rare in modern listings but occasionally shows up, usually on trucks or sport trims.
  • DCT (Dual-Clutch Transmission) — automated but uses two clutches for faster, sportier shifts; shows up on some performance trims.

How arbitration works with Manheim — if a green (or applicable yellow) vehicle has an issue that wasn't disclosed and falls within Manheim's arbitration policy (commonly things like undisclosed frame damage, odometer discrepancies, or a misrepresented title status), the buyer can file an arbitration claim within Manheim's specified window after the sale (often within a short number of days/mileage driven — always confirm current policy). Manheim reviews the claim against the original listing and condition report. Outcomes can include a price adjustment, the seller taking the vehicle back, or the claim being denied if the issue doesn't meet arbitration criteria. The key for reps: arbitration is a safety net for green/yellow vehicles, not a guarantee — and it's exactly why red-light vehicles are a different risk conversation entirely.

Timed sales vs. Simulcast Live — Manheim runs vehicles through different sale formats, and reps should know the difference:

  • Timed sale — a listing is open for bidding over a set window (often a day or more), and the highest bid at close wins — similar to an online auction-style listing. These often include a "Buy Now" button: a fixed price the seller will accept immediately, ending the auction on the spot for whoever clicks it first. Buy Now prices are typically set higher than where the vehicle would likely land in competitive bidding, since the seller is charging a premium for certainty and speed.
  • Simulcast Live — a live auction event where vehicles run one at a time through a lane in real time, with bidders (in-person and online) competing live, the way a traditional auction works. Bidding moves fast and is driven by who's actually in the lane that day.

Why we favor Simulcast Live: timed sales — especially the Buy Now option — tend to run at a premium because the seller is pricing in convenience and the buyer is paying to skip the uncertainty of competitive bidding. Simulcast Live is where real market price discovery happens, and it's typically where we find the better spread between MMR and what we actually pay. Timed sales aren't off the table, but Simulcast Live is the default lane we're hunting in.

Rep Mindset

You are an advisor, not a salesman. They came for a consultation. Confidence matters more than perfect wording. Don't rush.

Consultation Flow — 5 Sections

This is the live script structure. The Advisory Slideshow is an optional visual aid if it helps you stay on track, especially early on.

1
What DealerKey Auto Is

Start by asking about the car they're looking for — confirm what they wrote down is still accurate. Listen, make a genuine comment, let them talk.

Then explain: DealerKey isn't a traditional dealership — no lot, no inventory. We work directly with the client to find their specific car, and we don't charge anything unless we actually save them money — at least $3,000, usually more.

Pause and let that land.

Then explain the "why": dealerships buy in bulk from Manheim — the largest vehicle auction in the world (150,000+ cars available at any moment, 8M+ moving through annually). They mark cars up $4,000-$6,000 to cover the lot, salespeople, and financing department. We have access to that same auction, so we go straight to the source. Flat $1,500 advisory fee, collected only if we save the client at least $3,000.

Close the section: "Today I'm just going to show you what that actually looks like. At the end, you tell me if it makes sense for you."

2
Framing & Expectations

This section is short on purpose. The seed is already planted — now make sure the client feels zero pressure before seeing the data. Say it slowly and genuinely; it does a lot of work.

Core message: "My job today isn't to sell you on a specific car or push you toward any decision. I just want to show you what the market actually looks like — both what you can see as a regular buyer, and what's happening behind the scenes. We'll look at real numbers together. If at the end you'd rather keep searching on your own, totally fine."

3
The Website Search

This is where you build the most credibility — don't be afraid to spend several minutes here. Goal: give the client a frame of reference so they can see the value gap between the public market and DealerKey.

Pull up the car they're looking for with their budget. Point out that results are filtered to top-condition cars (4.0+), all clean titles with condition reports — and prices are already noticeably lower than local dealership listings.

Explain the AI angle: it's processed thousands of listings across the retail market — year, mileage, trim, title status — constantly updating, comparing every auction car to what that exact car sells for retail right now.

Point to the arbitrage number: that's the gap between dealership price and what we can actually get it for — what you'd pocket if you bought at auction and sold retail tomorrow. Let it land. Then: "And we haven't even gone into Manheim yet. This is just the surface layer."

4
Tour of Manheim

Your familiarity with the platform is the point — move confidently, narrate as you go. (Full Manheim platform training is covered separately — this is about how to present it.)

Set parameters live based on what the client told you — make, model, year, mileage range. Point out how much live inventory is available.

Select a listing with a solid condition grade (4.0+) and open the full listing: year, trim, confirmed mileage, condition grade.

Explain MMR — no fixed price since it's an auction, but MMR tells us what the car most likely sells for based on recent sales data. Work in out-the-door numbers: MMR + $1,500 fee + Manheim's usage fee (typically around $750) ≈ $2,250 on top, all-in total around [X].

Pull up the condition report and point out a minor item (e.g. a small dent) — this is the standard dealerships demand, and it works in the client's favor for the same reason. Show one or two more listings, then transition into Section 5.

5
Logistics & Closing

Don't let them leave thinking they found just another resource. The goal: they voluntarily hand the search over to you completely. Not a hard close — make it so obvious and easy they'd feel silly not to.

Explain the process: next 2-3 weeks, we handle the entire search. At least two check-ins per week, top 2-3 options from each new batch, with any standout highlighted clearly.

Soft Close #1 — "Does email or text work better for you?" Wait for the answer.

Explain the bid structure: when a car comes up, get on a call, build the bidding strategy together, set the ceiling bid ahead of time. Client contributes 25%, DealerKey covers 75%. Win the bid → client pays the rest, gets the keys. Don't win → 25% comes straight back, search continues. All of this in writing before any money moves.

Solidifying Close — "How much notice do you need before making a move? If I find the perfect car and the auction runs two days from now, can you move within 48 hours?" Wait for the answer.

Mention the option to watch a live auction if they're interested — it's genuinely cool to see.

Strongest Close — "For the next 2-3 weeks, let me handle it completely. You don't have to do a thing. If I haven't found the right one by then, no worries — we'll keep looking and you can jump back in too. Sound good?" Do not rush this. Their response tells you exactly how bought in they are.

Confirm all car details before ending the call.

The Three Closes

  • Soft Close #1 — "Does email or text work better for you?" Gets the client engaged and picturing the ongoing process. Wait for their answer before moving on.
  • Solidifying Close — "Can you move within 48 hours if the right car comes up?" Gets a commitment to their own readiness, not just to the process.
  • Strongest Close — "Let me handle it completely for the next 2-3 weeks, sound good?" Hands the search fully over to you. Don't rush this one — the response reveals how bought-in they really are.

Objection: "How do I know this isn't a scam?"

Track Four
Systems

Get comfortable in the tools you'll use every day — the CRM, Trello as backup, and the communication standards that keep everything consistent.

The CRM

Get familiar with the CRM — it's where all client notes live. Every client interaction should be logged. The level of detail matters less than whether it helps you (or another rep) find the client the right vehicle — don't over-engineer notes, just capture what's useful.

Update the CRM whenever you learn new info about what a client is looking for — preferences, budget changes, timeline shifts. The CRM is only as useful as it is current.

Login — use your DealerKey email, and only use that email for all client communication. Keeps everything traceable and professional.

Pipeline Stages

  • New — a lead that hasn't been successfully contacted yet.
  • Contacted — moved automatically by the CRM once a successful contact is logged. A contact attempt (unanswered call, unread message) doesn't move the stage — only an actual successful contact does.
  • Searching — client is ready and actively being sent listings.
  • Targeting — client has settled on a specific car and is preparing to bid. This is BAM territory — bid strategy, max bid, and the Vehicle Purchase Agreement all happen here.
  • Qualifying / Needs Time — client isn't ready to actively search yet (often waiting on financing).
  • Closed — deal complete.

Automatic follow-ups — the CRM automatically assigns follow-up reminders at intervals based on pipeline stage. These are defaults, not rules — feel free to edit follow-up timing as appropriate for the client, especially for "Qualifying / Needs Time" clients where the right cadence varies a lot person to person.

Trello — Backup CRM

Trello is the backup. Day-to-day, leave it alone — the CRM is the primary system. Refer to Trello specifically when a client has incomplete contact information in the CRM, since Trello may have it logged separately.

If the CRM ever fails or goes down, Trello is where client contact information is preserved as a fallback.

Step 3 — OpenClaw

What it is: OpenClaw is a framework that lets Claude operate more like an autonomous assistant — running on a schedule, monitoring for new information, and taking actions on your behalf rather than only responding when you message it directly.

Paired with Claude, OpenClaw turns Claude from "a chat you go to" into "an assistant working in the background" — drafting client messages, scanning new Manheim listings against your active clients' criteria, and keeping you updated without you having to manually check everything yourself.

Why it matters for reps: the bottleneck on how many clients you can serve well is usually attention — staying on top of every client's search, every new listing, every follow-up. OpenClaw doesn't replace the rep relationship, but it handles the repetitive monitoring and drafting so you can spend your time on the calls and decisions that actually need a human.

Ideas for how reps use it:

  • Automatically scan new Manheim exports against every active client's criteria and flag matches
  • Draft follow-up messages for clients in "Searching" based on their CRM notes, ready for the rep to review and send
  • Send the rep a daily or twice-weekly summary of what's new for each client
  • Track auction dates for clients with signed BAFs and proactively remind the rep before bidding windows close
📋 Setting this up takes a while and will involve some troubleshooting — that's normal. The key skill here isn't memorizing steps, it's learning to follow Claude Code's instructions closely. If you can do that, you can figure out almost anything.
